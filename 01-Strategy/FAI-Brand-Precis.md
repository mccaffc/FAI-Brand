# What FAI's Brand Is Now
## A synthesis from the brand audit, brand book v03, moodboard, Brand Direction proposal, and illustration library
*February 2026*

---

## The Organization in a Sentence

FAI is a tech-policy think tank that bridges Silicon Valley and Washington — convening Energy Secretaries and Y Combinator CEOs, throwing raves at DC nightclubs and hosting Supreme Court scholars at academic symposia — and has grown from a small libertarian tech advocacy group into arguably the most influential organization at the intersection of technology and conservative governance.

## The Brand's Personality

FAI's brand lives in a productive tension. It wants to be two things at once, and the interesting part is that it actually *is* both:

**Institutional gravity.** Major foundation funders (Bradley, Walton, Scaife, Hewlett, Arnold Ventures, Stand Together). Cabinet secretaries keynoting their summits. Actively staffing a presidential administration. Copyright symposia with academic panelists. This is a serious policy organization operating at the highest levels of American governance.

**Startup energy.** "Move fast and build things." A nuclear rave at Flash nightclub. Inc. Magazine calling them "the rave-throwing think tank shaping the tech-right." The origin story is Silicon Valley, not K Street. The people are technologists first, policy people second.

The brand's job is to hold both of these together without collapsing into either one. Too much gravity and you're just another Brookings. Too much startup energy and no Senate committee chair takes your white paper seriously.

## The Visual Identity System

### Palette

The primary palette is three colors with very specific values:

- **International Orange** (#FF4F00) — the signature. Not red, not vermillion, not burnt orange. A specific, saturated, forward-leaning orange that reads as energetic and slightly dangerous. It's closer to a hazard sign than a sunset.
- **Cod Gray** (#121212) — not true black. A warm near-black that's softer on screen and gives the dark backgrounds their particular character. This is the dominant background color across the brand.
- **Pure White** (#FFFFFF) — used for type on dark grounds, accent shapes, and breathing room.

The secondary and tertiary palettes extend into sky blue, amber/golden yellow, warm gray, and teal. These show up prominently in the illustration system but aren't as tightly codified in v03. The shades-and-tints system in the brand book defines systematic lightening/darkening scales for orange and gray, but the implementation rules (when to use which shade) aren't yet documented.

### Typography

Three typeface families, each with a distinct role:

**Schmalfette Grotesk** — the display face. An ultra-condensed grotesque that's used for the logotype and all large-scale display text (section headers in the brand book, event titles, posters). It's the face that gives the brand its visual punch. Usage rules: optical kerning, +20 tracking, limit to 2–3 lines and 2–3 words per line. It doesn't work for running text.

**IBM Plex Serif** — the workhorse. Used for headings and body text in publications. This is what people read when they read an FAI white paper or policy brief. The brand book specifies a type scale: 36pt/120% line-height for pull quotes, 21pt/130% for subheads, 10pt/140% for body. Bold for headings (12pt), Regular for body (11pt). The serif choice roots the brand in the IBM corporate design tradition — serious, engineered, but humane.

**IBM Plex Sans / Sans Condensed** — utility and support. Captions at 10pt Regular, footnotes in the Condensed variant at 10pt. This handles everything that needs to be there but shouldn't compete with the serif body text.

The brand book references a "pentatonic scale" approach to the type size hierarchy — this is the idea of using a limited set of predetermined sizes (like the five notes in a pentatonic musical scale) rather than arbitrary sizes. The specific sizes documented are 36pt, 21pt, 12pt, 11pt, and 10pt, which form the five "notes."

### The Logo

Three components that work independently and together:

- **The double chevron** (the "fast forward" logomark) — two right-pointing chevrons that read as a fast-forward symbol. Used as a standalone mark, as a page-corner device in the brand book, and as an accent element.
- **The logotype** — "FOUNDATION FOR AMERICAN INNOVATION" set in Schmalfette Grotesk, all caps, stacked two lines.
- **The lockup** — chevron + logotype together.

Three approved color combinations: all white on dark, all black on light, and white chevron + white type on orange. The brand book has thorough misuse documentation (no color mixing between mark and type, no stretching, no stylization, no busy image backgrounds).

### Illustration System

This is the most distinctive and developed visual asset FAI has. Built from a set of geometric/organic base shapes (documented in the `FAI Base Shapes.ai` file), the system produces compositions in two formats:

**Banner format** (2:1 landscape, 50+ variants) — edge-to-edge compositions for web headers, social covers, email banners. These are dense, full-bleed, and high-energy.

**Freestyle format** (1:1 square, 18 variants) — figure-on-ground compositions for social posts, podcast thumbnails, avatar-scale use. More focused and iconic.

The shape vocabulary includes: circles and semicircles, pod/lozenge/oval forms (the hero shape), concentric arcs and rings, vertical bar/stripe clusters, wave forms, and eye/vesica piscis shapes. All are flat, with no gradients, shadows, or dimensionality — consistent with the Swiss/modernist foundation.

The system has a "volume knob" quality: compositions range from monochrome (black and white with a single orange accent) to full-palette (five or six colors). This is the mechanism for bridging the institutional ↔ startup tension — you can dial the energy up or down depending on context.

### Layout and Grid

The brand book itself demonstrates the grid system: a three-column structure with a narrow left column for body text and annotation, and a wider right field for visual examples. Header bar across the top with three zones (section label, org name, page number). Orange section dividers for chapter breaks. The system alternates between dark grounds (Cod Gray) for statement pages and light grounds for detailed specification pages.

## The Voice

From the brand book's tone of voice section: professional, optimistic, authoritative but approachable. Context-sensitive — research papers and congressional testimonies sit at the formal end, op-eds and social content at the casual end. FAI has a separate written style guide (referenced but not included in the brand book).

The taglines capture the spectrum:
- **"No Innovation without Representation"** — the institutional, slightly polemical positioning
- **"Build Tech. Promote Freedom."** — the action-oriented, startup-energy version
- **"Move fast and build things."** — the internal mantra (a deliberate twist on Facebook's "move fast and break things")

## What's Working

The core visual identity is genuinely strong. The combination of Schmalfette Grotesk, International Orange, and the illustration system creates something that doesn't look like any other DC think tank — and that's exactly the point. The IBM Plex family for body text is a smart choice that nods to the heritage of American corporate design without being stuffy. The illustration system, built from modular base shapes, is the kind of infinitely generative toolkit that a one-person creative department needs.

## What's Missing

The brand book v03 documents the *elements* thoroughly (logo, colors, type) but drops off before documenting how to *use* them. Sections 5 (Iconography) and 7 (Application) are empty shells. There's no sub-brand architecture (how does Reboot relate to FAI visually?), no template specifications, no guidance for external vendors, no governance model, and no application examples beyond the illustrations themselves. The design system exists in Chris McCaffery's head; the brand book hasn't externalized it yet.

The legacy Lincoln Network artifacts (LinkedIn handle, email references, web footprint) remain uncleaned, which creates a fractured identity for anyone who encounters the old name alongside the new one.

And the biggest strategic gap: FAI's brand infrastructure was built for a small think tank. The organization's actual influence — Energy Secretaries, Senate committees, major foundation funding, Inc. Magazine profiles — demands an institutional-grade visual presence that the current website and fragmented sub-brand ecosystem don't deliver.
