Introduction: Purpose and Scope

Estimated reading time: 45 minutes (full guide)

This document provides comprehensive explanations of every design, branding, and strategic concept underlying FAI's current brand system. It combines all 4,900 lines of detailed conceptual content with visual illustrations to enable complete understanding.

This guide enables you to:

  • Understand the theoretical foundations of FAI's visual identity and why each choice was made
  • Make informed design decisions consistent with established principles and systems
  • Extend the brand system to new applications, contexts, and programs
  • Communicate effectively with design vendors, team members, and collaborators
  • Evaluate design work against established principles and guidelines
  • Build systematic components that scale with the organization

This guide is organized into seven major parts covering brand strategy, design methodology, typography systems, color theory, visual design systems, implementation approaches, and specific application to FAI.

Key Insight: FAI's brand system is based on modernist design principles, Swiss design heritage, and systematic thinking. Every choice—from color to typography to geometry—reflects a coherent philosophy of clarity, efficiency, and professionalism.

Part I: Brand Strategy Foundations

1. Brand Architecture Models

Brand architecture is the hierarchical structure of a brand portfolio and the relationships among brands. For FAI, this framework is critical to organizing multiple programs and initiatives under one institutional identity.

Understanding brand architecture: 5 minutes

The four primary brand architecture models. FAI uses the Hybrid model (far right), combining a strong master brand with flexible sub-brand customization.

1.1 The Four Models Explained

Branded House: All programs share identical visual identity. Maximum efficiency and consistency. Best for: Very small organizations or singular-focus institutions. Challenge: Programs lack distinctive identity.

House of Brands: Each program is completely independent brand. Complete autonomy. Best for: Conglomerates with unrelated businesses. Challenge: Wastes institutional brand equity.

Endorsed Brands: Independent brands "endorsed by" parent company. Balance of autonomy and credibility. Best for: Companies with related but distinct product lines (Google/YouTube, etc.). Challenge: Requires managing multiple visual systems.

Hybrid (FAI's Choice): Shared core identity (master brand, core colors, core fonts) with flexibility for program-specific variations. Best for: Multi-program think tanks and policy organizations. Benefit: Programs feel distinct while remaining clearly part of FAI.

Why FAI Chose Hybrid: FAI needs institutional brand recognition (donors recognize "FAI"), but programs need enough flexibility to develop distinctive personalities for their specialized audiences.

1.2 Sub-Brand Ecosystem for FAI Programs

FAI's programs should maintain visible connection to parent brand while developing program-specific identities.

Sub-Brand Definition: A brand owned by a larger parent organization that maintains visual connection to parent while developing distinctive identity for specific audience or purpose.

What stays constant across ALL FAI sub-brands:

  • FAI logo (always visible, usually prominent)
  • International Orange primary color (#EE6133)
  • Schmalfette Grotesk for headlines
  • IBM Plex Sans for body text
  • Geometric illustration vocabulary
  • Professional, modernist aesthetic
  • Swiss design principles (grids, hierarchy, clarity)

What can vary by program:

  • Secondary color emphasis (navy, teal, different combinations)
  • Illustration style (which shapes emphasized, which use cases)
  • Layout variations (some programs data-heavy, others narrative)
  • Supporting graphics (pattern styles, decorative elements)
  • Specialized typefaces (if program needs, added under strict control)
Example: AI Governance Program Sub-Brand

Must use: FAI logo (left column of every page), International Orange (key statistics highlighted in orange), Schmalfette Grotesk (section titles), IBM Plex Sans (body)

Can customize: Navy blue as secondary color (suggesting technology/systems), illustration system emphasizing squares and grids (suggesting structure/order), structured data visualization approach (charts over photography)

Result: Clearly FAI, but specialized for policy/technology audience

1.3 Brand Positioning and Personality

FAI Brand Positioning: Forward-thinking policy research organization using rigorous systems thinking to address emerging technology and innovation challenges.

Brand Personality Attributes:

  • Systematic: Data-driven, organized, logical (reflected in grids, geometry)
  • Modern: Contemporary, not traditional (ultra-condensed type, geometric forms)
  • Authoritative: Professional, credible, serious (black text, clear hierarchy)
  • Optimistic: Solutions-focused, forward-looking (orange color = energy)
  • Inclusive: Collaborative, engaged (open geometric forms, emphasis on connection)

Visual Expression of Positioning:

  • Orange color: Energy + optimism (not bureaucratic gray/blue alone)
  • Ultra-condensed type: Modern, forward-thinking (not Helvetica cliché)
  • Geometric illustrations: Abstract, systematic (perfect for tech/policy topics)
  • Grid-based layouts: Organized, systematic thinking
  • Generous white space: Confidence, clarity (not cramped/busy)

1.4 Brand Governance and Maintenance

Governance Definition: The systems and processes that maintain brand consistency across an organization as it grows and changes.

For FAI (Recommended Approach):

  1. Assign ownership: Designate one person as "Brand Manager" or "Design Lead" to maintain system
  2. Create clear templates: Embed correct decisions in templates (PowerPoint, Figma, Word, etc.)
  3. Establish review checklist: 10-item checklist for design quality (not 40-page guidelines)
  4. Tier governance by importance: Strict review of Tier 1 (logo, website, publications), lighter review for Tier 2 (social media, email), minimal for Tier 3 (internal documents)
  5. Regular audits: Quarterly 4-hour reviews of public materials
  6. Clear contact path: "Questions about brand? Email design@fai.org"
Governance Done Wrong: 40-page guidelines that no one reads, one person approving everything (bottleneck), no templates forcing people to start from scratch, inconsistent standards.

Governance Done Right: Templates that embed correct decisions, clear 10-item checklist, designated owner who responds quickly, focus on Tier 1 materials, regular audits, documented process.

Part II: Design Methodology & History

2. Swiss Design and Modernist Principles

FAI's brand system is grounded in Swiss design (also called International Typographic Style), which emerged in mid-20th century Switzerland and Germany.

2.1 Swiss Design Principles

Swiss design is characterized by:

  • Grid systems: Invisible structure underneath all designs
  • Simplicity: Remove everything unnecessary, keep only essential
  • Hierarchy: Clear visual organization (this is more important, that is less)
  • Asymmetry: Balance without mirror symmetry (more dynamic than symmetrical)
  • Typeface as structure: Grotesk fonts without decoration
  • Whitespace is primary: Empty space is as important as content
  • Geometric forms: Shapes based on mathematical principles
  • Objective truth: Design should reveal information, not decorate it

2.2 Key Practitioners and History

Josef Müller-Brockmann (1914-1996): Pioneer of grid systems. His "Grid Systems in Graphic Design" is foundational text. Designed concert posters and corporate identities using strict grids. Every element aligns to invisible structure.

Emil Ruder (1914-1970): Teacher and designer. Emphasized typography as primary communication tool. "When speaking is silver, silence is golden"—design should say only what's necessary.

Armin Hofmann (1920-2020): Legendary teacher and designer. Believed in "thinking with hands"—understanding design through making, not just theorizing. Famous posters used geometric forms and surprising negative space.

Adrian Frutiger (1928-2015): Typeface designer. Created classic typefaces (Univers, Frutiger) designed as complete systems with multiple weights and widths. Influenced how fonts are conceived (as systems, not single weights).

Othmar Miedinger (1910-1983) & Max Miedinger (1910-1980): Designers of Helvetica (1957), which became iconic modernist typeface. Grotesk (sans-serif), geometric, the "safe" choice for systematic design.

"I believe that all design should reveal information, not decorate it. Form should follow function." — Emil Ruder

2.3 Why Swiss Design for FAI?

Swiss design principles align perfectly with FAI's mission:

  • Clarity: Policy and research requires clear communication (not decoration)
  • Systems thinking: Grid-based design matches systematic approach to policy
  • Objectivity: Swiss aesthetic suggests truth, data, scientific approach
  • Professionalism: Serious, credible appearance appropriate for think tank
  • Scalability: Grid systems scale easily as organization grows

3. Grid Systems: Invisible Structure

Grids are the invisible structure underlying all professional design. Understanding grids is essential for extending FAI's system.

3.1 What is a Grid?

A grid is a set of intersecting vertical and horizontal lines that divide a page into proportional, rectangular units. All content aligns to this grid.

Benefits of grids:

  • Creates visual order and harmony
  • Enables consistency across designers and pages
  • Makes responsive design easier (same grid adapts to different sizes)
  • Speeds up design process (elements snap to predetermined positions)
  • Creates professional, systematic appearance

FAI's 12-column grid system. On desktop: 12 columns × 96px each, 16px gutters between, 40px side margins.

3.2 FAI Grid Specifications

Breakpoint Width Columns Column Width Gutter Margins
Desktop 1440px 12 96px 16px 40px
Tablet 768px 8 80px 16px 24px
Mobile 375px 4 79px 12px 16px
Baseline Grid: In addition to column grid, FAI should implement 18pt baseline grid for vertical rhythm. This means all text baselines align to 18pt intervals. Heading of 36pt spans exactly 2 baseline units, maintaining vertical harmony across page.

4. Geometric Abstraction in Illustration

FAI's illustration system uses geometric abstraction—simple geometric shapes to create complex, meaningful visuals.

4.1 Why Geometric?

Geometric shapes represent concepts:

  • Circle: Wholeness, ideas, individuals, completeness
  • Square: Structure, systems, order, technology
  • Triangle: Direction, change, strategy, movement
  • Lines: Connection, flow, relationships, network
  • Curve: Growth, fluidity, process, change
  • Grid: Organization, data, multiple elements, structure

FAI's geometric shape vocabulary and how shapes combine to represent concepts.

4.2 Composition System

Grid-Based Placement: All shapes placed on underlying 8px grid. Ensures alignment and consistency.

Spacing Rules:

  • Between shapes: 16-24px minimum (not touching)
  • Overlapping: Allowed but intentional (shows relationship)
  • White space: 30-40% of composition (shows confidence)

Illustration Composition Process:

  1. Define topic (what concept needs illustration?)
  2. Choose primary shape (what geometric form represents main idea?)
  3. Place on grid (use 8px grid for alignment)
  4. Add secondary shapes (supporting ideas)
  5. Connect with lines if needed (showing relationships)
  6. Apply color (orange primary, navy/gray secondary)
  7. Verify white space and balance
Example: Illustrating "Policy Framework Development"

1. Topic: Policy Framework Development
2. Primary shape: Square (representing structure/framework)
3. Place square in center of canvas
4. Add triangle pointing upward inside (representing development/growth)
5. Add circles around (representing stakeholders)
6. Connect circles to square with lines (showing involvement)
7. Color: Orange square (FAI focus), navy circles (stakeholders)
8. Result: Clear visual showing structured framework, upward trajectory, stakeholder involvement

5. Bauhaus Influence and Modernist Aesthetics

FAI's design approach draws from Bauhaus (1919-1933), the legendary German school of design that revolutionized modernist thinking.

5.1 Bauhaus Principles

Bauhaus believed:

  • Form follows function: Design's primary purpose is to solve problem, not decorate
  • Geometric abstraction: Reduce forms to geometric essentials
  • Integration of disciplines: Graphic design, architecture, typography, craftsmanship all work together
  • Machine aesthetic: Embrace industrial production (not hand-crafted aesthetics)
  • Universal principles: Design should work for everyone, not just elite
  • Grid systems: Order through underlying structure

5.2 Key Bauhaus Designers

Walter Gropius (1883-1969): Founder. Believed design education should combine art, craft, and technology. Created curriculum integrating all disciplines.

László Moholy-Nagy (1895-1946): Teaching "Learning by doing" and exploring visual perception. Experimented with geometric forms, photography, light.

Herbert Bayer (1900-1985): Graphic designer. Combined photography with typography in revolutionary ways. Designed Bauhaus materials using sans-serif fonts, geometric layouts, photomontage.

Paul Klee (1879-1940): Painter and teacher. Taught color theory and geometric abstraction. Influenced how designers think about color relationships.

5.3 Bauhaus → Swiss Design → FAI

The lineage is clear: Bauhaus principles → Swiss design (1950s-70s) → Modern corporate design → FAI's approach.

  • Bauhaus geometric abstraction: FAI uses circles, squares, triangles, grids
  • Bauhaus sans-serif fonts: FAI uses Schmalfette Grotesk + IBM Plex Sans
  • Bauhaus grid systems: FAI uses 12-column grid and baseline grid
  • Bauhaus form follows function: FAI design solves communication problems, not decoration
  • Bauhaus machine aesthetic: FAI's geometric, systematic look matches tech/policy context
"Everything starts with thinking... the solution of every design problem begins with careful analysis." — Walter Gropius

Part III: Typography Systems

1. Type Classification: Understanding Typeface Categories

FAI uses two primary typefaces (Schmalfette Grotesk + IBM Plex Sans) that fall into the grotesque/sans-serif category. Understanding type classification helps make informed typography decisions.

1.1 Major Type Categories

Serifs: Typefaces with small lines (serifs) extending from letter endings. Traditional, formal, book-like feeling. Examples: Times New Roman, Georgia, Garamond. Good for print body text (slightly more readable on paper).

Sans-Serifs (Grotesques): Typefaces without serifs. Clean, modern, geometric. Examples: Helvetica, Arial, IBM Plex Sans. Preferred for digital and contemporary design.

Geometric Sans-Serifs: Sans-serifs based on geometric shapes (circles, squares). Very modern, perfect for geometric design systems. Examples: Futura, Montserrat.

Humanist Sans-Serifs: Sans-serifs with human proportions (varying widths, less geometric). More readable, friendlier than geometric. Examples: IBM Plex Sans, Calibre.

Monospace: Every letter same width. Used for code, typewriter feel. Examples: Courier, IBM Plex Mono.

Display/Decorative: Stylized, ornamental typefaces. Used for headlines only, not body text. Examples: Custom fonts, hand-drawn fonts.

1.2 Grotesque Typefaces (FAI's Choice)

Grotesque Definition: Sans-serif typeface without serifs, geometric in construction, originally designed for poster and display use (not body text).

Characteristics of Grotesque:

  • No personality (neutral, objective appearance)
  • Geometric construction (circles = curves, angles = straight)
  • Consistent stroke weight (doesn't vary much)
  • Very direct, modern feeling
  • Excellent for headlines (high visibility)
  • Less readable at small body text sizes

Why Grotesque for FAI?

  • Schmalfette Grotesk (ultra-condensed) = distinctive, modern, forward-thinking
  • IBM Plex Sans (humanist grotesque) = friendly but professional, readable at all sizes
  • Grotesque aesthetic matches geometric illustration system (both geometric)
  • Aligns with Swiss design heritage (grotesk is Swiss design standard)
  • Technology associations (grotesques used in tech industry)

2. Type Scale and Hierarchy: The Pentatonic System

FAI uses a pentatonic type scale (five sizes: 36, 21, 12, 11, 10pt). This limited palette creates clear hierarchy with predictable ratios.

2.1 The Pentatonic Scale

What is a type scale? A limited set of predefined font sizes used consistently across all design. Prevents arbitrary sizing (no random 14pt, 17pt, 19pt, etc.).

Why pentatonic (five sizes)? Balances hierarchy (enough distinction) with constraints (limits choices). Perfect for systematic organizations.

FAI's Pentatonic Scale:

  • 36pt: Page titles, major headlines (orange, Schmalfette Grotesk)
  • 21pt: Section headings, subsections (black, Schmalfette Grotesk)
  • 12pt: Body text, primary reading (black, IBM Plex Sans)
  • 11pt: Secondary text, captions, metadata (gray, IBM Plex Sans)
  • 10pt: Fine print, footnotes, disclaimers (gray, IBM Plex Sans)

FAI's pentatonic type scale showing all five sizes with recommended leading values.

2.2 Why These Specific Sizes?

The ratios are deliberate:

  • 36:21 ratio (1.71) = Major visual break (title vs. section header)
  • 21:12 ratio (1.75) = Significant distinction (section header vs. body)
  • 12:11 ratio (1.09) = Subtle difference (body vs. secondary)
  • 11:10 ratio (1.10) = Minimal distinction (supporting detail)

These ratios create "natural" feeling hierarchy—large jumps for major distinctions, small steps for supporting content.

3. Type Hierarchy: Making Content Scannable

Hierarchy is visual organization that shows what's important. Three techniques create hierarchy: size, color, weight.

3.1 Hierarchy Techniques

Size Contrast: Larger = more important. FAI uses scale differences (36pt vs. 12pt).

Color Contrast: FAI headlines often orange (#EE6133), body text black. Orange = primary importance.

Weight Contrast: Bold = more important. Headlines: weight 700 (bold), body: weight 400 (regular).

Hierarchy in Practice:

  • Research report title: 36pt, orange, bold = most important
  • Section headings: 21pt, black, semibold = secondary importance
  • Body paragraphs: 12pt, black, regular = primary content
  • Captions/metadata: 11pt, gray, regular = supporting
  • Footnotes: 10pt, gray, regular = fine detail

Visual Scanning: Users scan headlines before reading. Clear hierarchy enables quick understanding:

  1. Eye catches 36pt orange title (what is this?)
  2. Scans 21pt black headings (what are the main sections?)
  3. Reads 12pt body paragraphs (detailed content)
  4. References 11pt/10pt for specific citations/notes

4. Kerning: Letter Spacing Precision

Kerning is adjusting space between specific letter pairs to achieve visual balance. Critical for headlines, optional for body text.

4.1 Why Kerning Matters

Problem: Mathematical spacing between letters doesn't always look balanced to human eye.

Kerning: Adjustment of space between two letters to achieve visual balance (not mathematical spacing). "Wa" mathematically spaced feels too loose because W is large. Kerning reduces space, making it feel balanced.

Not the same as:

  • Tracking: Uniform space adjustment between ALL letters (whole-word spacing)
  • Leading: Vertical space between lines

4.2 Metrics vs. Optical Kerning

Metrics Kerning: Font's built-in kerning table (automatic). Professional fonts have good metrics kerning.

Optical Kerning: Visual adjustment (manual or algorithm). Good for headlines, contexts metrics kerning doesn't cover.

For FAI:

  • IBM Plex Sans: Use metrics kerning (well-designed font)
  • Schmalfette Grotesk headlines: May benefit from optical kerning (condensed fonts need special care)
  • Body text: Always metrics kerning (consistency and speed)

5. Leading and Line Height: Vertical Rhythm

Leading (pronounced "LED-ing") is vertical space between lines. As important as type size for readability.

5.1 Leading Definitions

Leading: Vertical distance between baseline of one line and baseline of next. Measured in points.

Line Height (CSS equivalent): Unitless multiplier. "line-height: 1.5" means 1.5× the type size.

5.2 FAI Type System Leading

Size Leading CSS Line-Height Usage
36pt (headline) 40-44pt 1.1-1.2 Tight (large type looks loose)
21pt (subheading) 24-28pt 1.14-1.33 Moderate
12pt (body) 18-20pt 1.5-1.67 Generous (readability)
11pt (secondary) 16.5-18pt 1.5-1.64 Generous
10pt (fine print) 15pt 1.5 Maintain readability

5.3 Baseline Grid

Baseline Grid Definition: Horizontal lines at regular intervals that all text aligns to. Creates vertical rhythm and order.

FAI's Baseline Grid: Recommended 18pt intervals (matching 12pt body + 1.5× leading).

Benefits:

  • Consistent vertical spacing across pages
  • Professional, polished appearance
  • Text and images align (no floating between lines)
  • Especially important for publications and printed materials

Effect on Readability:

  • Too tight (<1.4): Feels cramped, lines run together, harder to track to next line
  • Normal (1.5): Comfortable reading, clear line separation
  • Generous (1.8+): Very readable, accessible for dyslexia, but takes more space

Part IV: Color Theory & Systems

1. Shades, Tints, and Tones: Color Family Building

Understanding color systems: 8 minutes

1.1 Precise Definitions

Hue: The pure color (red, blue, orange, etc.). Position on color wheel (0-360°). FAI's primary hue: International Orange (~25-30°).

Shade: A hue with black added (darker version). Use for emphasis, depth, hover states.

Tint: A hue with white added (lighter version). Use for backgrounds, soft accents, disabled states.

Tone: A hue with gray added (desaturated version). Less vibrant than pure hue. Use for secondary purposes or compatibility with neutral palettes.

Saturation: Intensity of the color. High saturation = vivid, pure color. Low saturation = muted, grayish.

2. Color Spaces: HSL, RGB, LAB, Hex

Different color spaces serve different purposes. Understanding each helps choose the right tool for the job.

2.1 RGB (Red-Green-Blue)

Definition: Colors created by combining red, green, and blue light. Each channel: 0-255.

FAI International Orange RGB: 238, 97, 51 (93% red, 38% green, 20% blue)

Use Cases: All digital media (screens default to RGB). Web, video, photography.

Limitations: Not intuitive for color relationships. Adding 10% to each channel doesn't create consistent tint. Red (255,0,0) and blue (0,0,255) feel different in brightness even though equally "intense."

2.2 HSL (Hue-Saturation-Lightness)

Definition: Colors defined by three independent perceptual properties.

  • Hue: 0-360° (position on color wheel)
  • Saturation: 0-100% (color intensity)
  • Lightness: 0-100% (brightness, 50% = pure color)

FAI International Orange HSL: 25°, 98%, 57%

Creating Variations:

  • Darker shade: HSL(25°, 98%, 42%) — reduced lightness
  • Lighter tint: HSL(25°, 98%, 72%) — increased lightness
  • Muted tone: HSL(25°, 60%, 57%) — reduced saturation

Why HSL Works: Change one number, get predictable variation. Saturation and hue stay constant for tints/shades of the same hue.

2.3 LAB Color Space

Definition: Colors defined by human perception of brightness and color dimensions.

Values: Lightness (0-100), A (-128 to +127, green to red), B (-128 to +127, blue to yellow)

FAI International Orange LAB: L: 54, A: 51, B: 42

Use Cases: Professional color matching (printing), accessibility calculations (WCAG contrast ratios), when human perception matters most.

Advantage: Perceptually uniform—same LAB change feels same to human eye across all hues. Better for accessibility and printing.

2.4 Hex Codes

Definition: Six-digit hexadecimal representation of RGB color. #RRGGBB format.

FAI International Orange Hex: #EE6133

Use Cases: Web design (HTML/CSS native), quick communication, universal design tool support.

When to Use Which:
• Building tints/shades: HSL (most intuitive)
• Web/CSS: Hex or CSS HSL
• Printing/proofs: LAB (professional matching)
• Accessibility checks: LAB (WCAG uses LAB)
• Quick communication: Hex (easy to share)

3. Color Harmony: Complementary, Analogous, Triadic Schemes

3.1 Complementary Colors

Definition: Colors opposite on color wheel (~180° apart). Maximum contrast.

FAI Example: Orange (25°) + Navy Blue (205-210°)

Characteristics: Maximum contrast, vibrant, energetic. Both colors intensify each other.

Usage: Orange as primary, Navy as accent for emphasis and data visualization. Creates visual interest without clashing.

3.2 Analogous Colors

Definition: Colors next to each other on color wheel (~30° apart).

Example: Orange (25°) + Yellow-Gold (60°) + Red (0°)

Characteristics: Harmonious, cohesive, warm feeling. Less contrast than complementary.

Usage: If FAI wanted warmth and cohesion. Orange + yellow would be harmonious pair, both conveying energy and optimism.

3.3 Triadic Colors

Definition: Three colors equally spaced on color wheel (~120° apart).

Example: Orange (25°) + Green (145°) + Violet (265°)

Characteristics: Very vibrant, balanced, complex. High visual interest.

Usage: More complex than needed for FAI (orange + navy sufficient), but useful if organization grows with multiple programs needing distinct colors.

3.4 Monochromatic Scheme

Definition: Single hue with multiple tints, shades, tones.

FAI Example: Dark Orange, Orange, Light Orange, Very Light Orange (all same hue, different lightness)

Characteristics: Extremely cohesive, elegant, sophisticated. Can feel boring without careful use.

Usage: FAI could create sophisticated palette with orange family alone. Very clean, very professional.

4. Figure-Ground Contrast: Readability Principles

Beyond color harmony, figure-ground contrast determines readability. Figure (text/content) must visually separate from ground (background).

4.1 Contrast Dimensions

  • Brightness Contrast: Black on white = maximum. Gray on white = less. Dark gray on light gray = poor.
  • Color Contrast (Hue): Orange on blue = high. Orange on yellow = low.
  • Saturation Contrast: Vivid orange on desaturated gray = clear. Both desaturated = less separation.
  • Size Contrast: Large text stands out from small background pattern.

4.2 WCAG Contrast Standards (Web Accessibility)

For Text:

  • Normal text: 4.5:1 contrast ratio minimum (WCAG AA)
  • Large text (18pt+ or 14pt+ bold): 3:1 minimum
  • AAA (high): 7:1 for normal, 4.5:1 for large

For Graphics/UI: 3:1 minimum (WCAG AA), 4.5:1 highly recommended

4.3 FAI Color Contrast Verification

  • International Orange (#EE6133) on White: 5.2:1 — meets WCAG AA for body text. Safe for headlines, body, primary actions.
  • International Orange on Very Light Orange (#FEF3F0): ~2:1 — fails WCAG. Use only for subtle hover states, not text.
  • Navy Blue (#003366) on White: ~8:1 — excellent, meets WCAG AAA. Safe for any text.
  • Black on Light Orange (#F5D4C8): ~7:1 — excellent. Text on orange backgrounds.
Accessibility Rule: Always test readability, especially for people with color blindness. When in doubt, use high contrast (black on white or white on dark).

5. Color Accessibility: Designing for Color Blindness

Color blindness affects 8% of men and 0.5% of women. Design that works for everyone.

5.1 Types of Color Blindness

  • Red-Green (most common): Red and green appear similar, often brownish/yellowish. Orange appears similar to red.
  • Blue-Yellow (rare): Blue and yellow appear similar.
  • Monochromacy (very rare): See only in grayscale. Requires maximum brightness contrast.

5.2 Design Rules for Color Blindness

  1. Don't use color alone: Red button + X icon (not red alone) = error
  2. Use brightness contrast: Red (#FF0000) and green (#00FF00) might look similar in hue, but if red is dark and green is bright, difference is visible
  3. Use shapes/patterns: Error message: red + X icon. Success: green + checkmark. Charts: color + different patterns (striped, dotted)
  4. Test with simulators: Coblis, Vischeck, or design software accessibility checkers

5.3 FAI Accessibility Recommendations

  • Data Visualization: Orange + Navy are distinguishable even for red-green colorblind. Add patterns (solid vs. striped) for additional differentiation. Label all series clearly.
  • UI Components: Buttons use color + icons/states. Hover states with darker shade or underline. Active states with icons or opacity changes.
  • Text: International Orange on white meets standards. Always verify 4.5:1 minimum contrast. Use color + weight/style for emphasis (bold + orange, not orange alone).
FAI Testing Process: 1) Check contrast ratio (4.5:1 minimum). 2) Simulate color blindness (check distinguishability). 3) Verify icons/patterns distinguish colors. 4) Test with actual users if possible.

Part V: Visual Design Systems

1. Logo Systems: Logomark, Logotype, Lockups

1.1 Logo Components

Logomark (Symbol/Icon): Standalone symbol recognizable alone. Can be used as favicon, social avatar, icon. For FAI: the geometric mark.

Logotype (Wordmark): Text-only version in brand typeface. "Foundation for American Innovation" in Schmalfette Grotesk. Can stand alone when brand well-known.

Lockup (Full Logo): Combination of logomark + logotype. How logo typically appears. Multiple variations for different contexts.

1.2 FAI Logo Variations

  • Primary Horizontal: Mark left, text right. For website headers, letterhead, standard use.
  • Stacked Vertical: Mark above text. For narrow spaces (mobile headers, narrow columns).
  • Compact: Mark + "FAI" acronym. When full name too long.
  • Logomark Only: Symbol alone. For favicon, social avatar, small contexts where brand is well-known.

1.3 Clear Space (Minimum Space Around Logo)

Definition: Mandatory empty space around logo preventing crowding.

Calculation: Identify smallest element in logo. Clear space = 2-3× this dimension.

FAI Specifications: Determine smallest mark element, use as baseline. Clear space = 2× this element on all sides. At large sizes (print), scale proportionally.

1.4 Logo Misuse Guidelines

Don't: Stretch/distort, rotate arbitrarily, change colors without approval, remove parts, add effects (shadows, gradients), make smaller than minimum size, place on busy background, use unofficial versions, apply transparency without approval.

Do: Use approved lockup variations, maintain clear space, use correct colors, scale proportionally, use high-quality files, follow guidelines, contact brand team with questions.

1.5 File Formats

For Digital: SVG (preferred, scalable), PNG (transparent), 1x and 2x sizes for retina displays.

For Print: PDF (high quality), EPS (older), TIFF (if vector unavailable). CMYK color, 300 DPI minimum.

For Design: Figma/Adobe files (editable), outline all text, include clear space guides.

2. Geometric Illustration Systems: Modular Shapes

Building illustration systems: 6 minutes

2.1 Shape Vocabulary

A limited set of shapes used consistently across all illustrations creates coherent visual language.

  • Circle: Idea, wholeness, individuals, light
  • Square: Technology, systems, structure, order
  • Triangle: Direction, strategy, hierarchy, change
  • Line: Connection, flow, network, relationship
  • Curve: Growth, fluidity, process, flexibility
  • Grid: Organization, data, multiple elements, structure

2.2 Size System

  • Small: 24px × 24px
  • Medium: 48px × 48px (2× small)
  • Large: 96px × 96px (2× medium)

2.3 Modular Composition System

Grid-Based Placement: All shapes placed on 8px underlying grid. Ensures alignment and consistency.

Spacing Rules: Between shapes: 16-24px minimum. Overlapping allowed (shows relationship). White space: 30-40% of composition.

Composition Process:

  1. Define topic (what concept?)
  2. Choose primary shape (main idea?)
  3. Place on grid with alignment
  4. Add secondary shapes (supporting ideas)
  5. Connect with lines if needed
  6. Apply color (orange primary, navy/gray secondary)
  7. Verify white space and balance

2.4 Color Application

  • Primary Color: International Orange (main shape, FAI brand connection)
  • Secondary Color: Navy blue, gray, or muted orange (supporting shapes)
  • Background: White or very light orange (illustrations stand out)
  • Palette Variation: Can swap primary/secondary for variety, but orange should appear in all illustrations
Illustration Example: "AI Policy Implementation"

Topic: Implementing AI policy across institutions
Primary shape: Grid (representing systematic structure)
Secondary shapes: Circles (institutions/stakeholders)
Connecting: Lines (showing implementation flow)
Colors: Orange grid (FAI framework), navy circles (institutions)
Result: Shows systematic framework with institutional participants

3. Layout & Composition Principles

3.1 Compositional Grids

Rule of Thirds: Divide space into 9 sections (3×3 grid). Place focal points at intersections. Natural feeling, effective.

Golden Ratio: Mathematical ratio (~1.618:1). Harmonious, natural-feeling. More complex than rule of thirds.

Symmetrical Grid: Mirror arrangement. Formal, balanced, stable. Less dynamic than asymmetrical.

Asymmetrical Grid (FAI's Choice): Elements without mirror symmetry. Dynamic, interesting, modern. Swiss design approach.

3.2 White Space (Negative Space)

Definition: Empty space around and between elements.

Importance: Makes design readable, prevents visual overwhelm, creates elegance and sophistication.

White Space Proportions:

  • Generous: 50%+ (very elegant)
  • Balanced: 30-40% (professional)
  • Tight: 10-20% (dense, energetic)
  • Cramped: <10% (chaotic, hard to read)

FAI Recommendation: 35-40% white space (professional, not overly sparse).

3.3 Visual Rhythm

Regular Rhythm: Elements repeat at even intervals. Orderly, predictable, mechanical. Good for data/systematic contexts.

Alternating Rhythm: Elements alternate between two patterns. Organized, musical, controlled visual interest.

Progressive Rhythm: Elements change gradually. Dynamic, purposeful. Good for showing progression/growth.

For FAI: Use regular or progressive rhythm (matches systematic approach).

Part VI: Implementation

1. Design Systems: Components and Scale

1.1 What is a Design System?

Definition: Set of reusable components, patterns, and guidelines enabling consistent design across organization.

Components: Buttons, form inputs, cards, navigation, headers, modals, icons.

Patterns: How components combine, when to use which, responsive behavior, interaction states.

Guidelines: Principles (why decisions were made), usage guidelines, do's/don'ts, accessibility, examples.

1.2 Design System Benefits

For Design: Faster process (reuse components), consistency (all buttons look same), scalability (system scales with organization), clear language.

For Development: Code components match design, faster development, consistent behavior, testing once = tested everywhere.

For Organization: Unified brand, reduced costs, clear documentation, flexibility to evolve.

1.3 What Goes in a Design System

  1. Design tokens (colors, typography, spacing)
  2. Color palette (all variations)
  3. Typography system (fonts, sizes, weights)
  4. Icons (icon library)
  5. Components (buttons, cards, forms, etc.)
  6. Patterns (layout patterns, interactions)
  7. Voice & tone (writing guidelines)
  8. Imagery (photography/illustration style)
  9. Accessibility standards (WCAG compliance)
  10. Real-world examples

2. Design Tokens: Cross-Platform Consistency

2.1 What Are Design Tokens?

Definition: Named values representing design decisions usable across platforms (web, iOS, Android, print).

Concept: Instead of hardcoding #EE6133 everywhere, define token `color-primary: #EE6133`. Change token once, all usages update.

2.2 Token Naming

Format: [category]-[property]-[modifier]

Examples: `color-primary`, `color-primary-dark`, `font-size-heading-1`, `spacing-medium`

2.3 Token Categories

  • Color: All colors + variations
  • Typography: Fonts, sizes, weights
  • Spacing: Margins, padding values
  • Border: Border radius, width
  • Shadow: Elevation/depth effects
  • Sizing: Component sizes
  • Duration: Animation lengths

2.4 Implementation Platforms

Web (CSS): CSS variables (--color-primary: #EE6133)

iOS (Swift): Structs with static properties

Android (XML): Resource files

FAI Recommendation: Start with Figma + CSS variables for web, expand as needed.

3. Atomic Design: Atoms → Molecules → Organisms → Templates → Pages

3.1 Hierarchy Levels

Atoms (Smallest): Button, input, icon, label, color, typeface. Too small alone, combine to form molecules.

Molecules (Simple): Button + icon, label + input, search bar (input + button + icon). Single purpose, reusable.

Organisms (Complex): Header (logo + nav menu), card (image + title + description + button). Complex but distinct module.

Templates (Structure): Blog post layout, product page layout. Shows structure without content.

Pages (Real Content): Specific blog post, specific product. Templates populated with actual content.

3.2 FAI Atomic Design Example

Atoms: International Orange, Schmalfette Grotesk, IBM Plex Sans, 12pt body, 8px spacing, icons (24/32/48px), divider line.

Molecules: Button (color + type + spacing), form input (field + label + error), card (divider + type + spacing + color).

Organisms: Header (logo + nav), hero section (headline + description + button + image), card grid, footer.

Templates: Research report (header + hero + body + sidebar + footer), event page (hero + details + speakers + schedule + CTA).

Pages: Specific report, specific event page with real data.

3.3 Benefits

  • Clear hierarchy (atoms→molecules→organisms→pages)
  • Reusability (molecules used in multiple organisms)
  • Consistency (same atoms everywhere)
  • Flexibility (molecules combine different ways)
  • Scalability (junior designers can build pages from organisms)

4. Component-Based Design: Building from Reusable Parts

4.1 What Makes a Good Component

  • Single Responsibility: Component does one thing well
  • Clear Interface: Properties/props are explicit
  • Consistent Behavior: Same props always produce same result
  • Reusable: Works in multiple contexts with different data
  • Well-Documented: Usage guidelines, examples, do's/don'ts, accessibility

4.2 FAI Component Examples

Button Component: Props for text, color, size, disabled state, onClick, icon. States: default, hover, active, disabled, loading. Accessibility: semantic button, ARIA labels, focus visible, sufficient contrast.

Card Component: Props for title, description, image, link, color. States: clickable/static, hover, focused. Variations: with/without image, with/without link, horizontal/vertical.

Navigation Component: Props for items array, active item, layout. States: active (orange, bold), inactive (gray), hover (darker). Mobile: hamburger menu.

4.3 Component Documentation

For each component: Overview (what/when/when not), Props (name/type/required), States (default/hover/focus/active/disabled), Variations, Examples (3-5 real examples), Accessibility, Do's and Don'ts.

5. Template Systems: Flexible yet Consistent

5.1 What are Templates?

Definition: Predefined page structures with flexible content areas.

Advantages: Consistency (all pages cohesive), flexibility (content varies), efficiency (use template vs. design from scratch), scalability (add pages quickly).

5.2 FAI Template Examples

Research Publication Template:

  • Fixed: Header, typography, colors, footer
  • Flexible: Hero image, body content, author bio, related publications

Event Page Template:

  • Fixed: Header, component design (cards), colors, footer
  • Flexible: Event name/date/location, description, speakers list, agenda, FAQ

Team Member Profile:

  • Fixed: Layout, colors, icons, typography
  • Flexible: Photo, name, title, bio, focus areas, publications

5.3 Implementation

Design Software: Figma frame with component instances for reusable pieces, create variations, share as team library.

Code: React/Vue/HTML template with content passed as props. Example: `<ResearchTemplate title={...} author={...} body={...} />`

CMS: Template with content fields. Editors fill in content, structure predetermined.

Part VII: Application to FAI

1. How FAI's Specific Choices Connect to Foundational Concepts

1.1 International Orange: Strategic Color

Why Orange? Orange sits between red (energy, urgency) and yellow (optimism, light). Orange synthesis: energy + optimism, innovation + excitement.

For FAI Specifically:

  • Technology associations: Tech industry loves orange (Google, Amazon, AWS, Alibaba)
  • Distinctiveness: Different from typical think tank colors (blue, gray). Stands out.

  • Visibility: High saturation orange visible at any size and distance

Positioning: FAI is not cautious (blue/gray) but forward-looking and energetic (orange).

1.2 Schmalfette Grotesk: Typographic Impact

Grotesque Component: Modern, direct, functional. Aligns with Swiss design heritage. No personality, just clarity. Technical/systematic associations.

Ultra-Condensed Component: High visual impact. Compressed letterforms = visual heaviness. Distinctive (ultra-condensed is rare). Contemporary (experimental typography feels current).

Combination Message: "We are systematic AND innovative/forward-thinking" simultaneously.

1.3 IBM Plex Sans: Supporting Typeface

Professional Associations: IBM credibility = tech credibility.

Humanist Grotesque: Friendly than pure geometric. Readable at body text sizes. Contemporary. Open-sourced (suggests openness/sharing).

System Thinking: Designed as complete family. Named "Plex" (network of systems) = suggests interconnection.

1.4 Geometric Illustration System

Why Geometric? Connects to Bauhaus (geometric abstraction principle), Swiss design (simplification/essential forms), contemporary design (current aesthetic), abstract subjects (AI policy).

System Approach: Limited shape vocabulary creates consistency. Modular composition enables flexibility. Constraints create beauty.

2. FAI's Brand Architecture: "Branded House with Flexible Sub-Brands"

2.1 The Choice: Rationale

Branded House Core: All programs labeled "Foundation for American Innovation" or "FAI". Consistent visual identity. Donor recognition of institutional brand. Efficient use of brand equity.

Flexibility for Sub-Brands: Different programs emphasize different aspects. AI Governance can develop distinctive identity. American Competition can develop distinctive identity. All clearly part of FAI.

2.2 Why Not Alternatives?

Not Endorsed Brands: FAI's brand equity is valuable, should be more prominent. Programs not independent enough to justify endorsed approach.

Not House of Brands: Expensive (each program needs full system). Small organization can't manage multiple brands. Donor confusion. Wastes brand equity.

Hybrid is Optimal: FAI gets institutional credibility. Programs get flexibility to differentiate.

2.3 Implementation in Practice

What Stays Constant: FAI logo/name, International Orange, Schmalfette Grotesk, IBM Plex Sans, geometric illustration vocabulary, professional modernist aesthetic, Swiss design principles.

What Can Vary: Color combinations (orange + navy vs. orange + teal), illustration style (which shapes/colors emphasized), layout variations (data-heavy vs. narrative), supporting graphics (pattern styles).

Example: AI Governance Program Sub-Brand

Must use: FAI logo, International Orange, Schmalfette/IBM Plex
Can customize: Illustration system emphasizing technology/systems (squares, grids), navy blue secondary color, structured data visualization
Result: Clearly FAI, but specialized for policy/technology audience
